AI in Education

Five high impact applications shaping the near future of teaching, learning, feedback, teacher capacity, and inclusive access.

Why this matters now

AI in education is no longer a future trend. The biggest recent story is the widening gap between how fast students and educators are adopting AI and how slowly schools are catching up with clear guidance, training, and evidence based oversight. The most consequential question has shifted from whether education will use AI to which uses actually improve learning.

Guiding idea

  • AI should support human learning, not narrow it.
  • Teachers remain central to judgment, care, and meaning.
  • Fairness, privacy, and access must be built into design.
  • Educational value depends on pedagogy, not technology alone.

Latest global signals, 2025 to 2026

Student use is rising fast

RAND reported in March 2026 that AI use for homework rose from 48 percent to 62 percent between May and December 2025, while 67 percent of students said AI use for schoolwork harms critical thinking.

Guidance still lags

Jobs for the Future reported in March 2026 that nearly seven in ten learners age 16 and older say AI is already incorporated into their coursework or training, yet many still learn about AI more from informal channels than from schools.

Higher education is effectively mainstream

Coursera’s February 2026 report found that more than 95 percent of surveyed students and educators reported using AI tools in an educational context, but only about one quarter of educators felt prepared to use AI effectively.

K to 12 adoption is already broad

CDT reported in 2025 that 86 percent of K to 12 students had used AI and 50 percent had used it for school related work, showing how normal AI assisted learning has already become.

1 Personalized Learning Paths

Right pace. Right support. Right time.

How AI helps students move through learning in ways that better match readiness and need.

Adaptive systems can guide different learners through different pathways while keeping the destination clear.
“AI powered personalized learning can make education more adaptive and timely, but its real value depends on ethical design, strong teaching, and equitable access.”
Core principle

Latest signal

Coursera’s 2026 report suggests AI use for personalized learning is now close to universal among surveyed higher education students and educators, which means the real differentiator is no longer access to AI itself, but whether institutions use it well.

The shift from one size fits all

Traditional classrooms often move many students through the same sequence, even though learners differ in pace, background, confidence, and support needs. AI driven personalization aims to make learning more responsive by adapting instruction to individual progress.

How it works

Adaptive systems can analyze quiz results, repeated mistakes, time spent on tasks, and progress patterns to estimate what a student has mastered and where support is needed. The system may then recommend review, extra practice, simpler explanations, or more advanced work.

Opportunity with responsibility

Personalization is powerful, but if the data is biased, incomplete, or poorly interpreted, a system may narrow opportunity, misjudge ability, or reinforce inequality. The strongest vision of personalized learning is both adaptive and ethically governed.

Selected sources: UNESCO. Artificial intelligence in education. OECD. Artificial intelligence and education and skills. Coursera. AI in Higher Education Report 2026.
2 Intelligent Tutoring Systems

Support that can meet confusion in the moment.

How AI tutors provide on demand guidance, practice, and feedback.

Intelligent tutoring systems can respond to student work in real time with hints, examples, and targeted practice.
“Immediate feedback changes the rhythm of learning. It turns confusion from a stopping point into the next step.”
AI tutoring perspective

Latest signal

RAND’s March 2026 findings suggest students are using AI for schoolwork more often while also becoming more worried about critical thinking. That makes the quality of tutoring design, not just availability, a core educational issue.

What they are

Intelligent tutoring systems are AI driven tools designed to provide individualized help through hints, explanations, worked examples, and guided practice. Unlike static software, they attempt to adjust support based on how a learner responds and where difficulty appears.

How they work

These systems often analyze student answers, repeated mistakes, timing, and progress patterns to decide what to do next. The next step may be a targeted hint, a review activity, a simpler explanation, or a new problem chosen to strengthen a weak skill.

Why it matters

The biggest advantage is immediacy. Students can receive support at the moment of confusion instead of waiting for the next class or office hour. That can reduce frustration, increase practice opportunities, and make learning more flexible and responsive.

The teacher still matters

AI tutors should support teachers, not replace them. Teachers remain essential for encouragement, judgment, social learning, and understanding needs that data alone cannot capture. The strongest use of AI tutoring is as a supplement to human teaching.

Real promise, real caution

Recent reviews show growing interest in AI driven tutoring systems in K to 12 education, and some studies report meaningful learning gains in specific settings. But the quality of evidence still varies, and not every tool delivers the same educational value. Good pedagogy matters as much as the model.

Selected sources: UNESCO. Artificial intelligence in education. OECD. Artificial intelligence and education and skills. RAND. Student Use of AI for Homework Rises as Concerns Grow About Critical Thinking Skills. Nature Digital Education.
3 Assessment and Feedback

Faster feedback is only valuable if it is meaningful.

How AI can support faster feedback, formative assessment, and more responsive teaching.

The real promise is not only automation, but feedback that arrives while learning is still unfolding.
“Assessment matters most when it helps students improve while there is still time to act on it.”
Formative assessment principle

Latest signal

A commercial but noteworthy signal came from Agilix in March 2026: its BusyBee grading system reported 3 million K to 12 submissions and said teachers approved AI suggested grades without edits 63 percent of the time. That suggests AI assisted grading is moving from experiment into workflow.

What it includes

AI can support automated scoring, writing feedback, rubric based review, progress analysis, and tools that help teachers interpret where students are struggling. These tools are most useful when they strengthen formative assessment rather than simply increasing testing.

How it works

Systems may analyze written responses, answer patterns, error frequencies, completion data, and learning trajectories to estimate what a student understands. The output can include feedback suggestions, highlighted misconceptions, or indicators that a learner needs review or intervention.

Rethinking assessment in the AI age

AI should not simply automate old assessment habits. The deeper question is not just how fast AI can score, but what we should value and measure. This is where fairness, transparency, and authentic demonstration of learning become central.

Selected sources: U.S. Department of Education. Artificial Intelligence and the Future of Teaching and Learning. UNESCO. The future of assessment in the AI age. Agilix BusyBee release, March 2026.
4 Teacher Support Tools

Reducing routine workload so human teaching can expand.

How AI can assist lesson design, summarization, planning, and insight generation.

The best teacher tools do not replace professional judgment. They give teachers more time to use it.
“A strong teacher with strong tools does more for learning than either one can do alone.”
Human centered AI

Latest signal

Coursera’s 2026 higher education report found that AI adoption is now mainstream among surveyed educators and students, yet only about one quarter of educators felt prepared to use AI effectively. The readiness gap may now matter more than the adoption gap.

What these tools do

Teacher support tools can help generate lesson outlines, summarize materials, create practice items, organize classroom data, and suggest instructional resources. They can also help teachers spot trends in student performance that may not be obvious at a glance.

Why they matter

Teacher time is one of education’s scarcest resources. When routine work takes too much time, teachers have less energy for discussion, mentoring, feedback, and deeper instructional design. AI tools can potentially shift some of that burden away from repetitive tasks.

Professional judgment still leads

AI generated material is only as useful as the teacher who reviews and adapts it. Teachers remain responsible for accuracy, alignment, context, and the human tone of instruction. Strong AI use in teaching is guided by professional judgment, not replaced by it.

What good integration looks like

The strongest models treat AI as a co pilot for preparation and reflection. Educators use it to accelerate drafting, compare options, and surface insights, then apply their own expertise to decide what is appropriate for learners.

Selected sources: U.S. Department of Education. Artificial Intelligence and the Future of Teaching and Learning. UNESCO. AI competency framework for teachers. OECD. Reimagining Teaching in an Accelerating World. Coursera 2026 report.
5 Enhanced Accessibility

Removing barriers without reducing inclusion to technology alone.

How AI can support more inclusive learning for students with diverse needs.

AI can support translation, speech tools, alternative formats, and more adaptive communication interfaces.
“Accessibility is not a feature added at the end. It is a design choice about who gets to fully participate.”
Inclusive design principle

Latest signal

Jobs for the Future’s March 2026 survey reinforces a central accessibility point: even as AI spreads through coursework and training, institutional guidance is still uneven. Access to AI alone is not the same as equitable support or meaningful inclusion.

What accessibility means here

In educational settings, accessibility includes helping students reach, understand, and interact with content in ways that fit their needs. AI can support this through reading assistance, speech tools, translation, alternative formats, and interfaces designed for learners with disabilities.

Why AI matters

Traditional accommodations can be costly, slow, or unevenly available. AI has the potential to make support more immediate, flexible, and personalized. That is especially important for learners who need multimodal access to content, communication, or assessment.

Inclusion is more than access

A student may technically receive content, but still be excluded from full participation if the broader learning environment is not inclusive. The best accessibility tools work alongside inclusive pedagogy, human support, and thoughtful design.

Selected sources: Institute of Education Sciences. AI-Augmented Learning for Individuals with Disabilities. UNESCO. AI and accessibility. Jobs for the Future, March 2026 survey.
Closing note

Future of Learning

The strongest recent evidence points in the same direction. AI in education has already become mainstream faster than governance, pedagogy, and safety practices have adapted. The most important next phase is not more adoption for its own sake, but sharper judgment about which uses actually deepen learning, widen opportunity, and protect students and teachers.
